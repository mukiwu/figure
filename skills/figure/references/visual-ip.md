# Hand-Drawn Rabbit IP

## Default Character

Use a small hand-drawn rabbit as the default visual IP:

- Soft oval rabbit head with two long simple ears at natural uneven angles. The head is only slightly larger than the torso, matching the user's preferred reference style; do not use an oversized toddler head.
- Fixed expressionless face: tiny uneven dot eyes, a small subtle nose, and a tiny off-center short horizontal dash mouth drawn as one imperfect hand-drawn pen stroke. The face stays neutral and almost blank in every image: no smile curve, no smirk, no x-shaped mouth, no blush, no cheek marks, no expression.
- Keep the character visually distinct from Miffy / Nijntje and similar classic minimalist picture-book rabbits. Avoid the exact combination of symmetrical upright ears, centered dot eyes, and an x-shaped mouth. Use the natural uneven ears, subtle nose, tiny off-center short horizontal dash mouth, small vest/shirt buttons, and hand-drawn editorial wobble as the distinguishing features.
- Do not use smiling, frowning, angry, shocked, crying, winking, or exaggerated facial expressions. The rabbit's humor and emotion must come from body pose, props, timing, and action, not from the face.
- 兔子的視角可依構圖變化：正面、四分之三側面、側面、背影、俯視或低角度皆可。無論角度如何，臉部仍維持無表情。
- When composing a new image, actively choose a camera angle that fits the idea, and vary it across generations. Do not repeatedly use the same side-facing or three-quarter profile when another angle would work.
- Stable body model: a small hand-drawn rabbit with loose editorial proportions, like the user's preferred reference image. Keep a soft oval head only slightly larger than the torso, a small shirt-shaped torso, short thin arms, short thin legs, and small rounded hands and feet. The silhouette is compact, light, and wobbly, but not babyish or huge-headed. Avoid a chubby, heavy, muscular, athletic, broad-shouldered, adult-built, or oversized-toddler-head body.
- Simple short-sleeve shirt, tiny open collar, short pants, or tiny vest with two to three small buttons when clothing helps readability.
- Gender presentation is flexible. If the passage clearly implies a gendered role or point of view, adjust clothing, hair silhouette, posture, or small accessories subtly to match; otherwise keep the rabbit gender-neutral.
- Curious, focused, cute, and a little strange in an editorial way; the body can feel small, short, and adorable, but the idea and gesture should stay intelligent rather than babyish.
- Creative gestures are welcome only when the body mechanics stay simple and compact: both feet planted, sitting, kneeling, lightly leaning on a prop, or using a nearby stool or tabletop. Keep actions close to the torso and readable at thumbnail size.
- The rabbit has short thin arms, so do not ask it to reach across the composition. For writing, drawing, highlighting, connecting dots, or moving cards, place the target object close to the rabbit. The elbow should stay bent or close to the torso, and the hand should usually stay within about one head-width of the body.
- Prefer stable short-arm actions: tap, stamp, pin, underline, circle, slide, nudge, hold a small card close to the chest, press a button, point with a short pointer, or connect two nearby dots with a short line.
- Avoid long-arm actions and poses that often deform the character: pulling distant threads, stretching over a page, climbing, dangling, balancing on one foot, twisting the torso, lunging, or holding tools far away from the body.
- Keep the humor restrained. Do not make the rabbit random, slapstick, meme-like, babyish, or incoherent.
- Uneven black marker/pen outlines, imperfect strokes, hand-drawn wobble.
- Minimal facial details; no realistic fur texture.
- Mostly black-and-white by default. Use color only as small muted clothing color blocks on the rabbit. Clothing color patches should vary naturally across images among muted dusty blue, warm gray-beige, soft sage green, muted lavender-gray, or pale clay. Do not default to green. The color blocks should feel like Korean editorial illustration patches, and must leave a thin white gap between the colored fill and the black line art. Do not add blush, rosy cheeks, or cheek marks; keep the face clean and expressionless. If a little texture is ever needed, use a few small hatch marks on clothing or props, never on the face.

## Character Consistency Lock

When writing every rabbit prompt, include a concise consistency lock so image generation does not drift:

```text
Character consistency lock: keep the exact same little rabbit in every image — a small hand-drawn rabbit with loose editorial proportions, close to the user's preferred reference style. The head is a soft oval or rounded oval, only slightly larger than the torso, never an oversized toddler head; two long simple rabbit ears with natural uneven angles; small shirt-shaped torso with a tiny open collar or two small buttons; short thin arms and short thin legs, small rounded hands and feet, light wobbly hand-drawn posture. The body is compact and light, not chubby, not bulky, not muscular, not broad-shouldered, and not adult-built. Face stays neutral and expressionless: tiny uneven vertical dot eyes, a small subtle nose if needed, and a tiny off-center short horizontal dash mouth drawn as one imperfect hand-drawn pen stroke; no x-shaped mouth, no smile curve, no smirk, no blush, and no cheek marks. Do not enlarge the head; keep the head-to-body ratio like the reference image: cute, balanced, and small rather than big-headed.
```

Use this lock even when the rabbit is shown from the back, side, overhead, or low angle. Camera angle and pose may vary, but the body proportions must remain recognizable.

## Composition Style

- Pure white background.
- Loose diagram language: arrows, boxes, brackets, dotted paths, small labels, simple icons.
- Black line art for the diagram and labels, with occasional light gray hatching if needed for hierarchy. Only the rabbit's clothing may have small muted color patches.
- One image should contain one central structure. Leave out secondary systems, long checklists, and full process documentation.
- Avoid colored diagram shapes, colored arrows, colored backgrounds, gradients, shadows, rendered 3D, glossy vector style, stock illustration style, and polished mascot branding.
- Keep the rabbit central enough that its action is readable at thumbnail size.

## IP Participation Test

Before finalizing a shot, verify:

- If the rabbit is removed, does the diagram lose its main action?
- Is the rabbit manipulating the concept rather than pointing at finished information?
- Does the rabbit's pose match the cognitive verb?
- Are the arms still short, with hands close to the body and no stretched writing hand?
- Is the pose stable rather than twisted, lunging, or contorted?
- Does the rabbit match the preferred reference proportions: soft oval head only slightly larger than the torso, small shirt-shaped body, short thin limbs, tiny off-center short horizontal dash mouth, and no oversized toddler head?
- Is the image still about one core structure rather than many related ideas?
- Does the rabbit feel inventive without becoming childish or random?

Revise the shot if any answer is no.
